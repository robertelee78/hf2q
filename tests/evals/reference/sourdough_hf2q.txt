Making sourdough bread is a blend of science (fermentation) and art (handling dough). Because it relies on wild yeast rather than commercial yeast, the process is slower, but the flavor and texture are superior.

Here is a comprehensive guide to making a classic **Sourdough Boule (Loaf).**

---

### Phase 1: The Essentials (Equipment & Ingredients)

**The Tools:**
*   **Digital Scale:** Essential for precision. Measuring by volume (cups) is unreliable in bread making.
*   **Large Mixing Bowl:** For fermenting dough.
*   **Dutch Oven:** For baking. This traps steam, which is vital for a crispy crust.
*   **Proofing Basket (Banneton) or a Bowl with a Towel:** To hold the shape.
*   **Lame (or a very sharp razor blade):** To score the dough.
*   **Dough Scraper:** To help handle sticky dough.

**The Ingredients:**
*   **Active Sourdough Starter:** 100g–200g (fed and bubbly).
*   **Flour:** High-quality Bread Flour (or a mix of Bread and Whole Wheat).
*   **Water:** Filtered water is best (chlorine can inhibit yeast).
*   **Salt:** Fine sea salt (avoid iodized table salt).

---

### Phase 2: The Timeline (The 24-Hour Workflow)

Sourdough cannot be rushed. A typical timeline looks like this:
1.  **Morning (8:00 AM):** Feed the starter.
2.  **Midday (12:00 PM):** Mix dough.
3.  **Afternoon (1:00 PM – 5:00 PM):** Bulk Fermentation (The rise).
4.  **Evening (6:00 PM):** Shaping.
5.  **Overnight:** Cold Proof in the fridge.
6.  **Next Day:** Bake.

---

### Phase 3: Step-by-Step Instructions

#### 1. The Levain/Starter Prep
Ensure your starter is at its peak. It should be bubbly, smell slightly fruity/acidic (not purely sour), and pass the **"Float Test"** (a small amount of starter floats in water if it's active).

#### 2. Autolyse & Mixing (The "Rough Mix")
*   **Combine:** Mix your flour and water until no dry clumps remain. 
*   **Rest:** Let this sit for 30–60 minutes. This hydrates the flour and begins gluten development.
*   **Add Starter & Salt:** Add your active starter and salt. Incorporate thoroughly.

#### 3. Bulk Fermentation (The First Rise)
This is the most critical stage. You are building structure through **"Stretch and Folds."**
*   Instead of kneading (which can be aggressive), perform "Stretch and Folds." 
*   Every 30 minutes, wet your hands, grab the side of the bowl, pull the dough up high, and fold it over itself. Repeat for all four sides.
*   Do this 4 times (roughly 2 hours total).
*   **Goal:** The dough should become smooth, elastic, and increase in volume by 50–75%.

#### 4. Shaping (The Final Form)
*   **Pre-shape:** Gently turn the dough onto a floured surface. Shape it into a loose ball. Let it rest for 20 minutes (this is the "Bench Rest").
*   **Final Shape:** Use the dough scraper to tension the dough. Flip it over, tighten it into a tight boule, and place it seam-side up in a floured proofing basket.
*   **The Goal:** You want a tight "skin" on the surface of the dough to prevent it from flattening like a pancake.

#### 5. Cold Proofing (The Secret to Flavor)
Place your shaped dough in the fridge (covered) for 12–24 hours. This cold fermentation slows down the yeast but allows the bacteria to produce complex, sour flavors. It also makes the dough firmer and easier to score.
