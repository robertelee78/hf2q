Making sourdough bread is a blend of science (fermentation) and art (shaping and intuition). This guide covers everything from managing your starter to the final bake.

---

### Phase 1: The Tools & Ingredients

**Essential Equipment:**
*   **Dutch Oven:** Crucial for creating steam, which ensures a crispy crust.
*   **Digital Scale:** Sourdough is about ratios; measuring by volume (cups) is imprecise.
*   **Mixing Bowl & Spatula:** For dough.
*   **Proofing Basket (Banneton) or a bowl lined with a tea towel:** For the final rise.
*   **Lame (or a very sharp razor blade):** For scoring the dough.
*   **Bench Scraper:** For handling sticky dough.

**Core Ingredients:**
*   **Active Sourdough Starter:** Bubbling, fed, and doubled in size.
*   **Flour:** High-quality bread flour (or a mix of bread and whole wheat).
*   **Water:** Filtered is best (chlorine can inhibit yeast).
*   **Salt:** Fine sea salt (avoid iodized table salt).

---

### Phase 2: The Timeline (The 24-Hour Workflow)

Sourdough cannot be rushed. A typical schedule looks like this:
*   **Day 1 (Morning):** Feed the starter.
*   **Day 1 (Afternoon):** Mix dough.
*   **Day 1 (Evening):** Bulk Fermentation (first rise).
*   たDay 2 (Morning): Shape and final proof.
*   Day 2 (Bake): The oven.

---

### Phase 3: Step-by-Step Instructions

#### 1. The Autolyse (Optional but recommended)
Mix your flour and water until no dry clumps remain. Let it sit for 30–60 minutes. This hydrates the flour and begins gluten development.

#### 2. Adding Starter and Salt
Add your active starter and salt to the autolyse dough. Mix thoroughly. This is the "incorporation" phase.

#### 3. Bulk Fermentation (The First Rise)
This is the most critical stage. You aren't just waiting; you are building structure.
*   **Stretch and Folds:** Instead of kneading, perform "stretches and folds" every 30 minutes for the first 2 hours. Wet your hands, grab the side of the dough, pull it up, and fold it over itself. Repeat for all four sides.
*   **The Goal:** The dough should transform from a shaggy mess into a smooth, elastic ball that holds its shape.
*   **The End Point:** You are looking for a 50–100% increase in volume. It should look bubbly and "jiggly."

#### 4. Shaping (Pre-shape and Final Shape)
*   **Pre-shape:** Gently turn the dough onto a floured surface. Shape it into a loose ball. Let it rest for 20 minutes (this is called "bench rest").
*   **Final Shape:** This creates tension. Use your hands to pull the dough toward you on a clean surface, tightening the "skin" of the dough. Place it upside down into a floured banneton.

#### 5. The Cold Proof
Place your shaped dough in the fridge (uncovered or covered) for 8–24 hours. This cold fermentation develops that signature "sour" flavor and makes the dough easier to score.

#### 6. Scoring and Baking
*   **Preheat:** Heat your Dutch oven in the oven to 450°F (230°C).
*   **Score:** Turn the cold dough onto parchment paper. Use a lame to make one deep, decisive cut. This controls where the bread expands.
*   **The Bake:**
    *   **Phase 1 (Lid ON):** Bake for 20–25 minutes. The steam trapped in the pot creates the "oven spring" (the rise).
    *   **Phase 2 (Lid OFF):** Remove the lid, reduce heat to 400°F, and bake for another 20 minutes until the crust is deep golden brown.

### Phase 4: Troubleshooting Common Issues

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Flat/Dense Bread** | Weak starter or under-proofing. | Ensure starter is very active; extend fermentation time. |
| **Dense/Gummy Center** | Underbaked or cut too early. | Ensure internal temp reaches 205°F
