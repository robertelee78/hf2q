Making sourdough bread is a journey of managing living organisms (wild yeast and bacteria). Unlike commercial yeast, which is predictable, sourdough relies on timing, temperature, and fermentation.

This guide covers the four essential stages: **The Starter, The Dough, The Fermentation, and The Bake.**

---

### Phase 1: The Starter (The Engine)
Before you bake, you need a "levain" (active starter). If your starter isn't bubbly and doubling in size within 4–6 hours of feeding, it isn't ready.

**The Golden Rule:** Only use your starter when it is at its **peak activity**.

---

### Phase 2: The Ingredients & Tools
*For one standard loaf:*

**Ingredients:**
*   **Active Sourdough Starter:** 100g (fed and bubbly)
*   **Warm Water:** 350g (filtered is best; chlorine can inhibit yeast)
*   **Bread Flour:** 500g (High protein is essential for structure)
*   **Fine Sea Salt:** 10g

**Essential Tools:**
*   Digital kitchen scale (measuring by volume/cups is inaccurate for bread)
*   Large mixing bowl
*   Dough scraper (optional but helpful)
*   Proofing basket (Banneton) or a bowl lined with a linen towel
*   Dutch Oven (essential for creating steam)
*   Lame (or a very sharp razor blade)

---

### Phase 3: The Process (Step-by-Step)

#### 1. Autolyse (The Hydration)
Mix your water and starter in a bowl until combined. Add the flour and mix until no dry clumps remain. Cover with a damp cloth and let rest for **30–60 minutes**.
*   *Why?* This allows the flour to fully hydrate and begins gluten development without effort.

#### 2. Adding Salt
Sprinkle the salt over the dough. Use your hands to dimple the salt in and squeeze the dough until fully incorporated.

#### 3. Bulk Fermentation (The Rise)
This is the most critical stage. Instead of "kneading" like traditional bread, we use **"Stretch and Folds"** to build strength.
*   **The Technique:** Wet your hand. Reach under one side of the dough, pull it upward, and fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl and repeat 4 times.
*   **The Schedule:** Perform 4 sets of stretches, spaced **30 minutes apart** (Total time: 2 hours).
*   **The Rest:** Let the dough sit undisturbed for another 2–4 hours.
*   **Goal:** The dough should increase in volume by about 50–75%, look aerated, and have visible bubbles on the surface.

#### 4. Preshape and Bench Rest
Gently tip the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Fold the edges toward the center to create a loose ball. Let it rest on the counter for **20–30 minutes**. This relaxes the gluten so it doesn't fight you during the final shaping.

#### 5. Final Shape
This is where you create "surface tension."
*   Pull the dough toward you on the counter, creating tension on the "skin" of the dough.
*   Flip it over so the seam is up. Roll it tightly into a log (batard) or a ball (boule).
*   Place the dough upside down (seam side up) into a floured proofing basket.

#### 6. Cold Proof (The Flavor Builder)
Cover the basket with plastic and place it in the refrigerator for **12–24 hours**.
*   *Why?* Cold fermentation slows down yeast but allows lactic acid bacteria to thrive, creating that signature "sour" tang and better digestibility.

---

### Phase 4: The Bake

#### 1. Preheating
Place your Dutch Oven inside the oven and preheat to **450°F (230°C)**. You want the pot screaming hot.

#### 2. Scoring
Carefully turn your cold dough out onto a piece of parchment paper. Use a razor blade (lame) to make one deep slash (about 1/2 inch deep) across the top.
*   *Why?* This acts as an "exhaust valve," controlling where the bread expands so it doesn't burst at the sides.
