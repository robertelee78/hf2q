Making sourdough bread is a journey of managing living organisms (wild yeast and bacteria). Unlike commercial yeast, sourdough requires patience, temperature control, and intuition.

This guide is broken down into four phases: **The Starter**, **The Dough**, **The Fermentation**, and **The Bake**.

---

### Phase 1: The Starter (The Engine)
Before you bake, you must have an active, bubbly sourdough starter. If your starter is "young" (less than 2 weeks old), it won't have enough strength. If it is mature, it should double in size within 4–6 hours of feeding and smell fruity/tangy, not like gym socks.

**The Golden Rule:** Only bake when your starter is at its **peak** (maximum height and bubbly).

---

### Phase 2: The Ingredients & Tools
*For a standard loaf:*
*   **Active Starter:** 100g (fed and bubbly)
*   **Warm Water:** 350g (filtered is best; chlorine can inhibit yeast)
*   **Bread Flour:** 500g (High protein is essential for structure)
*   **Fine Sea Salt:** 10g

**Essential Tools:**
*   Digital kitchen scale (volume measurements like "cups" are too inaccurate for sourdough)
*   Large mixing bowl
*   Bench scraper
*   Proofing basket (Banneton) or a bowl lined with a linen cloth
*   Dutch Oven (essential for creating steam)
*   Lame (or a very sharp razor blade)

---

### Phase 3: The Process (Step-by-Step)

#### 1. The Autolyse (Mixing)
Mix your water and starter in a bowl until dissolved. Add the flour and salt. Mix by hand until no dry flour remains. It will look shaggy and messy.
*   **Rest:** Cover with a damp cloth and let rest for 30–60 minutes. This allows the flour to hydrate and begins gluten development.

#### 2. Bulk Fermentation (The First Rise)
This is where flavor and air are built. Instead of "kneading" like traditional bread, we use **"Stretch and Folds."**
*   **Stretch & Fold:** Every 30 minutes, grab one side of the dough, pull it upward, and fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl and do this 4 times (North, South, East, West).
*   **Repeat:** Do 4 sets of folds (total of 2 hours).
*   **Rest:** Let the dough sit undisturbed for another 2–4 hours. 
*   **Goal:** The dough should increase in volume by about 50–75%, look bubbly on the surface, and feel "jiggly" like a bowl of gelatin.

#### 3. Shaping (Creating Tension)
*   **Pre-shape:** Gently tip the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Fold the edges toward the center to create a loose ball. Let it rest for 20 minutes (Bench Rest).
*   **Final Shape:** Flip the dough over. Pull the dough toward you across the counter to create surface tension. The skin of the dough should be taut. 
*   **The Tension:** If the dough is floppy, you haven't created enough tension. A tight skin prevents the bread from spreading like a pancake in the oven.

#### 4. Cold Proof (The Flavor Builder)
Place your shaped dough upside down into a floured proofing basket (Banneton). Cover with plastic to prevent drying.
*   **The Fridge:** Place in the refrigerator for 12–24 hours. This "cold retard" slows down the yeast but allows the bacteria to produce that signature sour flavor.

#### 5. The Bake (The Transformation)
*   **Preheat:** Place your Dutch Oven inside the oven and preheat to **450°F (230°C)** for at least 45 minutes.
*   **Score:** Take the dough out of the fridge. Flip it onto parchment paper. Use a razor blade to make one deep slash (about 1/2 inch deep) across the top. This directs the expansion.
*   **Steam:** Carefully lower the dough into the hot Dutch Oven. Cover with the lid. The lid traps the moisture escaping from the dough, creating steam—this is the secret to a crispy, shiny crust.
*   **The Two-Stage Bake:**
    1.  **Lid ON:** Bake for 20–25 minutes.
    2.  **Lid OFF:** Bake for another 20–25 minutes until the crust is deep
