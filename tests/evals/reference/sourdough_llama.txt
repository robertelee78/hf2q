Making sourdough bread is a blend of science (fermentation) and art (handling dough). Because it relies on wild yeast rather than commercial yeast, the process is slower, but the result is a superior flavor and texture.

This guide covers the **"No-Knead" Method**, which is the most effective way for beginners to achieve a professional crust and airy interior.

---

### Phase 1: The Essentials
To succeed, you need the right tools and ingredients.

**The Ingredients:**
*   **Active Sourdough Starter:** 100g–200g (must be bubbly and active).
*   **Bread Flour:** 500g (High protein is key for structure).
*   **Water:** 350g–375g (Filtered water is best; chlorine can inhibit wild yeast).
*   **Salt:** 10g (Fine sea salt).

**The Tools:**
*   A Dutch Oven (essential for steam).
*   A digital scale (volume measurements like "cups" are too imprecise for bread).
*   A proofing basket (Banneton) or a bowl lined with a heavy linen towel.
*   A sharp razor blade (Lame) or a sharp knife.

---

### Phase 2: The Timeline (The 24-Hour Workflow)

#### 1. The Levain/Starter Activation (Morning - 8:00 AM)
Before you bake, ensure your starter is "active." Feed your starter and wait until it has doubled in size and passes the "float test" (a small piece of starter floats in water).

#### 2. The Autolyse (The Mix)
*   **Combine:** Mix your flour and water in a large bowl until no dry flour remains. It will look shaggy.
*   **Rest:** Cover and let sit for 30–60 minutes. This hydrates the flour and begins gluten development.

#### 3. Adding Starter and Salt (The Incorporation)
*   Add your active starter and salt to the dough. 
*   **The Technique:** Use your hands to "squish" the dough together. Squeeze the dough through your fingers until it is fully incorporated. It will feel messy, but this is normal.

####ま 4. Bulk Fermentation (The First Rise)
This is the most critical stage. You are building structure through **"Stretch and Folds."**
*   **The Process:** Instead of kneading, perform 4 sets of "Stretch and Folds" every 30 minutes. Wet your hand, grab the side of the dough, pull it up high, and fold it over the center. Repeat for the whole bowl.
*   **The Goal:** After 4 sets, let the dough rest undisturbed. The dough should grow in volume and look "bubbly" or aerated.
5.  **The Rule of Thumb:** If the dough doubles in size and feels jiggly like a marshmallow, it’s ready for the next step.

#### 5. Shaping (The Tension)
*   Turn the dough onto a floured surface. 
*   **Pre-shape:** Gently fold the dough into a rough ball. Let it rest for 20 minutes (this is the "bench rest").
*   **Final Shape:** Use the dough to create tension. Tighten the surface of the dough by pulling it toward you on a clean surface. The goal is a tight "skin" on the top of the loaf.
*   **The Proof:** Place the dough in a floured proofing basket. Cover with a damp cloth or plastic.

#### 6. The Cold Proof (The Flavor Builder)
*   Place the basket in the fridge for 8–24 hours. 
*   **Why?** Cold fermentation slows down the yeast but allows the bacteria to produce lactic acid, creating that "sour" flavor and making the bread easier to digest.

---

### Phase 3: The Bake (The Heat)

1.  **Preheat:** Place your Dutch Oven in the oven and heat to **450°F (230°C)** for at least 45 minutes.
2.  **The Score:** Turn the cold dough out onto a piece of parchment paper. Use a razor (Lame) to slice a deep 1/2-inch cut through the top. This controls where the bread expands.
3.  **The Bake (Stage 1 - Steam):** Lower the dough into the hot Dutch Oven. Cover with the lid. Bake for **25 minutes**. The lid traps the steam from the dough, which is vital for a "springy" rise and a crispy crust.
